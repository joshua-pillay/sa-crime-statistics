	\section*{Abstract}
	\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Abstract}
	
	This report presents an exploratory, spatial, and predictive analysis of South African Police Service crime statistics spanning 12 years across nine provinces and seven crime categories. The analysis of crime statistics is a necessity for supporting existing frameworks in an effort to address the current South African crimescape. A multi-tool pipeline spanning Excel, Python, SQL, MATLAB and Power BI was used for EDA, geospatial analysis, trend decomposition, and time series forecasting. The analysis revealed the dominating provinces and their associated geographic crime concentration, the prevalent crime categories and the annual national crime patterns across the full analysis period. Near-term forecasting revealed no likely decline in projected figures without structural intervention. The analysis and its findings may support existing frameworks by potentially informing a data-driven approach to address South Africa's crime burden.

	\section{Introduction}
	Crime is regarded as a national priority in South Africa and consistently ranks among the top five countries with the highest recorded crime index scores (\cite{numbeo}). As such, crime remains one of the most significant challenges to the country's economic development and social stability, with strategic policies being implemented to explicitly identify security and safety as a foundational condition for inclusive growth. In an effort to track progress, the South African Police Service (SAPS) publishes annual crime statistics as their primary public accountability mechanism (\cite{saps2023}). \\\\
	Given the analytically-rich nature of the published multi-year SAPS crime statistics, this report documents an in-depth data analysis of the national and provincial crime figures over a 12-year period, employing an multi-tool pipeline spanning Excel, Python, SQL, MATLAB, Power BI. The analysis was conducted to better understand the nature of crime in South Africa from temporal, spatial, and categorical perspectives, such as identifying which provinces and crime categories dominate, characterising the overall annual trend, and assessing how findings can inform future policing strategies. Particular attention is paid to the influence of anomalous periods on reported crime figures, notably the suppression of crime counts during the COVID-19 lockdown of 2020.

	\subsection{Background \& context}
    South Africa's crimescape is characterised by persistently high levels of violent and contact-related offenses, with contact crimes (particularly murder, assault and robbery) consistently accounting for the largest share of serious community-reported crime incidents ($\sim41\%$) across the annual SAPS reporting periods (\cite{iss}). Despite several crime categories being characterised by modest declines over certain periods, reported contact incidents remained persistently high and even resurged following the COVID-19 lockdown period (\cite{iss}). Furthermore, the geographical distribution of crime in South Africa is far from uniform; crime is concentrated in urban, economically active provinces, with Gauteng and Western Cape consistently recording the highest absolute crime totals (\cite{saps2023}). However, this geographic concentration is a reflection of urbanisation and socioeconomic inequality rather than inherently higher criminality, which further complicates straightforward provincial comparisons. South Africa's governance response to its crime burden is formalised through several strategic frameworks, most notably Chapter 12 of the National Development Plan (NDP) 2030 and the SAPS Strategic Plan 2025 -- 2030. The NDP 2030 plan explicitly identifies security and safety as a precondition for inclusive growth, and the SAPS Strategic Plan 2025 -- 2030 explicitly endorses a data-driven approach to targeting crime occurring in high-risk areas (\cite{ndp2030}; \cite{saps2025}). These frameworks emphasise the relevance of structured, evidence-based analysis of SAPS crime statistics to national policing priorities. The national COVID-19 lockdown caused a significant anomaly in the crime statistics data for 2020, where an artificial decline was observed across most categories due to the suppression of both criminal activity and crime reporting (\cite{faull2021}). However, given that the crime figures rebounded towards pre-pandemic levels as restrictions eased, that decline in figures cannot be treated as a genuine improvement in public safety, and is explicitly flagged as a data anomaly in the interpretation of results. 
	
	\subsection{Research questions \& motivation}
	The analysis of the South African crime statistics data for the 12-year period is structured around the following research questions and their motivation:
	
	\subsubsection*{\textbf{Research Question 1 (RQ1):}}
	\begin{itemize}
		\item Which provinces have the highest overall crime figures, and how do they compare to one another? 
		\item Motivation: The need to understand how provinces of varying population sizes and socioeconomic profiles influence geographic inequality in crime burden. 
	\end{itemize}
	
	\subsubsection*{\textbf{Research Question 2 (RQ2):}}
	\begin{itemize}
		\item Which crime categories are most prevalent, and have they been increasing or decreasing over time?
		\item Motivation: The need to understand the growth or decline of different crime types, and how that behaviour directly informs policy decisions made by SAPS for resource allocation. 
	\end{itemize}
	
	\subsubsection*{\textbf{Research Question 3 (RQ3):}}
	\begin{itemize}
		\item Are there identifiable cyclical or long-term patterns in national crime data across the 12-year window?
		\item Motivation: The need to seek out patterns that could inform operational deployment planning for communities.
	\end{itemize}
	
	\subsubsection*{\textbf{Research Question 4 (RQ4):}}
	\begin{itemize}
		\item Can a reasonable short-term forecast be produced for a selected crime category? 
		\item Motivation: The need to demonstrate a forecasting application, producing actionable near-term projections that align with SAPS Annual Performance Plan planning cycles. 
	\end{itemize}

	\section{Data \& Methodology}
	
	\subsection{Dataset description}  \label{dataset_descrip}
	The dataset utilised in the analysis was publicly sourced from Kaggle and is derived from the official annual crime statistics reports published by SAPS (\cite{kaggle}; \cite{saps2023}). The raw dataset, in CSV format, includes the crime figures for the financial period 2011 to 2022 across nine provinces and seven crime categories, including the national crime figures for each year-category pair. As such, there is a total of (9 provinces + 1 national) $\times$ 7 categories $\times$ 12 financial years = 840 row entries in the dataset. The dataset comprises four variables: \texttt{Geography}, \texttt{Crime Category}, \texttt{Financial Year}, and \texttt{Count}. 
	
	\subsubsection*{\texttt{Geography}} 
	A text-type dimension variable that describes one of South Africa's nine provinces. The province names appear as abbreviations in the raw dataset, which are listed as follows: \texttt{EC} (Eastern Cape), \texttt{FS} (Free State), \texttt{GT} (Gauteng), \texttt{KZN} (KwaZulu-Natal), \texttt{LIM} (Limpopo), \texttt{MP} (Mpumalanga), \texttt{NC} (Northern Cape), \texttt{NW} (North West), \texttt{WC} (Western Cape), and \texttt{ZA} (national level).
	\subsubsection*{\texttt{Crime Category}} 
	A text-type dimension variable that describes one of seven broad categories in the South African crimescape. SAPS classifies crime incidents according to the following criteria: serious crimes reported through community, and crimes detected through police action (\cite{iss}). Community-reported serious crimes are further divided into three main categories: violent or contact crime, property-related crimes, and other serious crimes. Given the significant contribution of certain contact offences to the overall crime landscape, contact crimes are further divided into the following categories: contact crimes, robberies with aggravating circumstances, sexual offences, and contact-related crimes. Therefore, the seven broad categories that appear in the raw dataset are as follows:
	\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
		\item \texttt{Aggravated Robberies} --- Robbery involving a weapon or aggravating circumstances.
		\item \texttt{Contact Crimes} --- Interpersonal violent offences involving direct physical contact with the victim.
		\item \texttt{Contact Related Crimes} --- Arson and malicious damage to property.
		\item \texttt{Crimes Detected as a Result of Police Action} --- Offences surfaced through active police operations.
		\item \texttt{Other Serious Crimes} --- Various forms of theft including stock theft and commercial offences.
		\item \texttt{Property Related Crimes} --- Burglary, vehicle theft, and related property offences.
		\item \texttt{Sexual Offenses} --- Rape, sexual assault, and related offences.
	\end{enumerate}
	It is noted that each of the seven broad crime categories has its own subcategories and corresponding raw CSVs, but these are not relevant to the analysis.
	\subsubsection*{\texttt{Financial Year}} 
	A text-type dimension variable that describes one of twelve entries in the financial year spanning a 12-year period (2011 -- 2023). Since SAPS defines their financial year as 1 April to 31 March the following year, the entries appear in the form \texttt{20**/20**}, and are listed as follows: \texttt{2011/2012}, \texttt{2012/2013}, $\ldots$ , \texttt{2021/2022}, \texttt{2022/2023}. 
	\subsubsection*{\texttt{Count}} 
	The integer-type measure variable that represents the reported crime incidents for each province-category-year trio. 
	\subsubsection*{Limitations}   \label{limitations}
    \begin{itemize}
    	\item The dataset contains absolute crime figures rather than per-capita rates, meaning that the crime figures reflect population size and density rather than individual crime risk.
    	\item The dataset is aggregated at the provincial level and therefore lacks police station-level crime figures, preventing any sub-provincial spatial analysis. 
    	\item The \texttt{2020/2021} reporting period is characterised by an artificial suppression of crime figures due to the COVID-19 national lockdown, rendering these figures unrepresentative of the underlying crime trend (\cite{faull2021}).
    \end{itemize}
	
	\subsection{Analytical pipeline}
	The data analysis was conducted across eight stages, with each stage implementing a designated tool in a distinct, non-overlapping role within the analytical pipeline. The nine stages (and their associated tools) constituting the analytical pipeline are as follows:
	
	\subsubsection*{Stage 1 --- Data profiling \& transformation (Excel)} \label{stage1}
	Stage 1 comprised the initial inspection of the raw dataset with Excel. After reading the raw CSV file into Excel, a data quality inspection was conducted, which entailed reviewing data structures, labels, types and other possible quality issues the dataset may have contained. An exploratory summary containing first-pass provincial and categorical totals was produced via pivot table building. Additionally, two transformations were applied: filtering out national-level rows (\texttt{ZA}) and sorting the three dimensions in ascending order.
	
	\subsubsection*{Stage 2 --- Data cleaning \& preparation (Python)} \label{stage2}
	Stage 2 involved the formal cleaning of the raw dataset \texttt{sa{\_}crime{\_}raw.csv} in \texttt{01{\_}cleaning.ipynb} using \texttt{pandas}. This included verifying data quality findings from Stage 1 programmatically, renaming columns, label entries, and parsing strings to integers. Thereafter, the cleaned dataset \texttt{sa{\_}crime{\_}cleaned.csv} was exported as a new CSV for use in subsequent pipeline stages. An outline of the data cleaning steps is provided in \Cref{data_cleaning}. 
	
	\subsubsection*{Stage 3 --- EDA (Python)} \label{stage3}
	In Stage 3, EDA was performed in \texttt{02{\_}eda.ipynb} using \texttt{matplotlib} and \texttt{seaborn}. The EDA phase comprised four visualisations: provincial crime distribution chart (with per-capita caveat noted), annual national trend chart (with COVID-19 anomaly annotated), category trend line chart, and a crime category heatmap, with each visualisation addressing RQ1 -- RQ3.
		
    \subsubsection*{Stage 4 --- Geospatial analysis (Python)} \label{stage4}
    In Stage 4, two choropleth maps were produced in \texttt{03{\_}geospatial.ipynb} using \texttt{geopandas} and the Natural Earth Admin-1 shapefile (\cite{naturalearth}). One map displays the cumulative provincial crime totals across the full analysis period, while the other displays a snapshot of the provincial crime totals for the most recent year. The choropleth maps, which address RQ1, were produced using a yellow-green colour map. 
    
    \subsubsection*{Stage 5 --- Trend decomposition (MATLAB)} \label{stage5}
    In Stage 5, a moving average decomposition was applied in \texttt{trend{\_}analysis.mlx} to isolate the underlying crime trend from year-to-year noise at the national level for \texttt{Contact} crime, addressing RQ3. A 3-year moving average was applied by computing the mean of each three consecutive annual observations. As such, the moving average decomposition performed a smoothing of short-term fluctuations to reveal the longer-term direction of travel. A moving average was selected for its simplicity, interpretability, and suitability for a short annual time series, effectively smoothing the fluctuations without making assumptions about the data.
    
    \subsubsection*{Stage 6 --- Time series forecasting (Python)} \label{stage6}
    In Stage 6, an \texttt{ARIMA(1,1,1)} model was fitted to \texttt{Contact} crime at a national level in \texttt{04{\_}arima.ipynb} for forecasting purposes. The model fitting was performed using \texttt{statsmodels} (\cite{seabold2010}), producing a 3-year projection with a 95\% confidence interval (CI), addressing RQ4. AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average (ARIMA) models are statistical models that use past values and past errors as the basis for forward-looking projections (\cite{box2015}). The \texttt{ARIMA(1,1,1)} model incorporates one autoregressive term, one degree of differencing to achieve stationarity, and one moving average term. The \texttt{ARIMA(1,1,1)} model was selected for its suitability to univariate time series data, its ability to handle non-stationarity through differencing, and its interpretability as a concise model appropriate for a short annual time series.
   
    \subsubsection*{Stage 7 --- Structured querying (SQL)} \label{stage7}
    Stage 7 comprises the establishment of an SQLite database from \texttt{sa{\_}crime{\_}cleaned.csv} and querying using DB Browser for SQLite. SQL scripts \texttt{schema.sql} and \texttt{queries.sql} (six analytical queries) were used to establish the table schema and extract several aggregated views of the data, such as provincial totals, category breakdown by year, and annual incident trends, addressing RQ1 -- RQ3. The analytical queries also serve a cross-validation role against EDA results. The extracted views feed directly into the Power BI dashboard.    
    
    \subsubsection*{Stage 8 --- Dashboard (Power BI)}  \label{stage8}
    Stage 8 comprises the building of an interactive dashboard using Power BI. The dashboard is fed by SQL-aggregated views from Stage 7, enabling end-user exploration of provincial and category-level crime figures across the analysis period. The dashboard is available in the Appendix and \texttt{crime{\_}dashboard.pbix} in the \href{https://github.com/joshua-pillay/sa-crime-statistics}{\texttt{sa{\_}crime{\_}statistics}} repository. 
	
	\subsection{Data quality, cleaning, \& preparation} \label{data_cleaning}
	Data quality assessment of the raw CSV was initially performed in Stage 1 using Excel, with findings subsequently verified programmatically in Stage 2 using \texttt{pandas}. Additionally, several cleaning steps were applied to \texttt{sa{\_}crime{\_}raw.csv} in Python to produce \texttt{sa{\_}crime{\_}clean.csv}, preparing the data for subsequent use in the analytical pipeline. 
	\subsubsection*{Quality assessment \& transformations (Excel)}
	Upon inspection of the raw CSV in Stage 1, it was determined that:
	\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
		\item The data types and labels were consistent, and contained no missing, duplicate or blank entries. Several zero entries were found across raw subcategory-level datasets, but were deemed potentially legitimate as no crime figures may have been reported for that particular province-subcategory-year trio.   
		\item The original dataset comprised both provincial and national crime figures for each category and financial year. To produce a dataset with only provincial-level figures, national-level row entries were filtered out, with the remaining figures being sorted in ascending order according to the three dimension variables. Consequently, the total row entries of the raw dataset were reduced to 9 provinces $\times$ 7 categories $\times$ 12 financial years = 756.
		\item Two discrepancies were identified: (1) 190201 count difference between national and provincial figures for \texttt{Property Related Crimes} category due to the erroneous entry of the \texttt{2021/2022} national total into the \texttt{2021/2022} EC province-level figure; rectified by inserting the correct value of 14789 at EC province level; (2) 588 count difference between national and provincial figures for \texttt{Crime Detected as a Result of Police Action} category due to misaligning source-level publications; treated published national value as authoritative figure, so no action was taken.
		\item The following changes were subsequently applied in Stage 2: renaming column labels, province entries, category entries, and parsing year entries to 4-character integers.
	\end{itemize}
	
	\subsubsection*{Cleaning steps (Python)}
    After exporting the filtered \texttt{sa{\_}crime{\_}raw.csv} from Excel and importing it into Python, Stage 2 comprised the programmatic verification of Stage 1 data quality findings, and the following cleaning steps:
    \begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item Renaming of column labels: \texttt{Geography} to \texttt{province}, \texttt{Crime Category} to \texttt{crime{\_}category}, \texttt{Financial Year} to \texttt{financial{\_}year}, and \texttt{Count} to \texttt{incident{\_}count}.   
    \item Mapping of \texttt{province} abbreviation entries to their full names.
    \item Removal of redundant suffix \texttt{Crimes} from relevant \texttt{crime{\_}category} entries. Furthermore, the \texttt{Crimes Detected as a Result of Police Action} category was renamed as \texttt{Police Action}.
    \item The parsing of \texttt{financial{\_}year} text string entries to the first four characters. which yields the first year entry of the financial year as an integer. This was adopted for analytical convenience and consistency with standard time series indexing conventions.
    \end{itemize}
    In addition to the cleaning steps, Stage 2 also created the relational database \texttt{sa{\_}crime.db} populated with the clean dataset, which served as the database used in Stage 7. A summary of the variables in \texttt{sa{\_}crime{\_}clean.csv} is provided in \Cref{dataset_tbl}. For the full scope of the quality assessment and analytical steps taken in Stages 1 and 2, consult \texttt{data{\_}profiling.xlsx} and \texttt{01{\_}cleaning.ipynb} in the \href{https://github.com/joshua-pillay/sa-crime-statistics}{\texttt{sa{\_}crime{\_}statistics}} repository.

	\subsubsection*{Provincial crime distribution (RQ1)} 
	\Cref{fig:province_bar} presents the total reported crime incidents per province across the full analysis period, addressing RQ1.

The Northern Cape consistently recorded the lowest crime totals. However, it should be noted that the reported crime figures represent absolute counts and reflect provincial population size and density rather than per-capita crime rates, meaning that provinces with larger populations will naturally record higher absolute totals.
	
	\subsubsection*{Annual national trend (RQ3)}
	\Cref{fig:national_trend} presents the trend in national crime figures across the full analysis period, addressing RQ3.  
	\begin{figure}[H]
		\centering
		\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{national_crime_trend.png}
		\caption{National crime trend with COVID-19 annotation.}
		\label{fig:national_trend}
	\end{figure}
	\noindent In \Cref{fig:national_trend}, national crime incidents are formatted to millions, and the COVID-19 lockdown period is explicitly annotated. Additionally, a linear trendline was fit to the annual national figures for observing the overall crime trend. From \Cref{fig:national_trend}, several observations are made: the 2011 -- 2013 period recorded a slight increase in national crime figures, followed by a relatively stable 2013 -- 2016 period. From 2016 -- 2019, a slight decrease was recorded in national crime, followed by a sharp decline in 2020. From 2020 -- 2022, a considerable increase was recorded in national crime figures. The linear trend is characterised by a downward slope, and is thus representative of a general decrease in the overall crime trend over time. It should be noted that despite 2020 (COVID-19 lockdown) recording the lowest national crime total in the analysis period, the anomaly reflects an artificial crime suppression, rendering the figures unrepresentative of the underlying crime trend. 
	   
	\subsubsection*{Category trend lines (RQ2)}
	\Cref{fig:top5} presents the trend in national crime figures for the top five crime categories across the full analysis period, addressing RQ2. 
	\begin{figure}[H]
		\centering
		\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{top5_categories.png}
		\caption{National trend of top 5 crime categories (2011 -- 2022).}
		\label{fig:top5}
	\end{figure}
	\noindent In \Cref{fig:top5}, the crime incidents are formatted to thousands, and each crime category is explicitly labelled. From \Cref{fig:top5}, it is evident that contact crimes recorded the highest crime figures across the analysis period, followed by property-related crimes and other serious crimes. Contact-related crimes recorded the lowest figures among the top five categories. Overall, four of the five categories followed a relatively similar crime trend, with the exception of \texttt{Police Action}; while the other categories broadly declined over reporting period 2011 -- 2017, \texttt{Police Action} increased steadily before declining sharply. Nonetheless, each category was characterised by two specific features: the minimum crime figure recorded in 2020 (COVID-19 lockdown) and a general increase in crime figures from 2020 to 2022.

	\subsection{Trend decomposition}   \label{trend_decomposition}
	The trend decomposition performed in Stage 5 produced one visualisation addressing RQ3, with the visual derived from \texttt{sa{\_}crime{\_}clean.csv} using MATLAB. For a full outline of the sequence followed for the trend decomposition, consult \texttt{trend{\_}analysis.mlx} in the \href{https://github.com/joshua-pillay/sa-crime-statistics}{\texttt{sa{\_}crime{\_}statistics}} repository.\\\\ 
	\Cref{fig:trend_decomp} presents the moving average trend decomposition of national contact crime figures across the full analysis period, addressing RQ3. 
	\begin{figure}[H]
		\centering
		\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{trend_analysis.png}
		\caption{Moving average trend decomposition for national contact crimes (2011 -- 2022).}
		\label{fig:trend_decomp}
	\end{figure}
    \noindent \Cref{fig:trend_decomp} comprises three components: the actual annual series, the 3-year moving average, and a linear trend fitted to the actual figures, with total crime incidents formatted to thousands. The COVID-19 lockdown period is also explicitly annotated. Given that the total figures for contact crimes were the highest of all categories (see \Cref{fig:top5} and \Cref{fig:heatmap}), it is plausible that the national contact crime aggregate is heavily weighted towards the dominating provinces, and is thus a suitable representation of the annual national trend.\\
    From \Cref{fig:trend_decomp}, it is evident that the actual figures remained relatively stable for the 2011 -- 2019 period, with approximate incidents ranging between 600,000 and 625,000. Additionally, the 3-year average smoothed the fluctuations (see \Cref{stage5}), softening the already-stable figures for the 2011 -- 2019 period. The linear trend is characterised by a nearly flat, slightly downward-facing slope, indicating an overall stability in national contact crime across the full analysis period.\\\\
    The transition in actual figures to 2020, which is indicative of the COVID-19 period, is characterised by the sharpest single-year drop of approximately 85,000 incidents. The 3-year moving average dropped by approximately 25,000 incidents between 2018 and 2020, softening the dip in actual figures and confirming it as a temporary anomaly rather than a structural change in crime. A rebound in crime figures for both actual and smoothed series is observed post-2020, with actual contact crimes recording the highest figure in 2022.\\\\
    Overall, the underlying trend in contact crimes beneath year-to-year noise is characterised by a broadly stable but slightly declining path for the 2011 -- 2019 period. Additionally, the sharp rebound in crime figures post-2020 is indicative of a temporary crime suppression rather than a structural one. The linear trend slope confirms that the contact crime series sustained no long-term increase or decrease across the full analysis period.

The figure comprises three components: the actual historical series, the \texttt{ARIMA(1,1,1)} projection, and the 95\% CI, with crime incidents formatted to thousands. The COVID-19 lockdown period is also explicitly annotated. For the logic dictating the choice of contact crime series, see \Cref{trend_decomposition}. Furthermore, the historical series in \Cref{fig:forecast} is consistent with the figures and trend observed in \Cref{fig:top5} and \Cref{fig:trend_decomp}. 
	\begin{figure}[H]
		\centering
		\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{forecast_chart.png}
		\caption{3-year forecast using \texttt{ARIMA(1,1,1)} for national contact crimes (2011 -- 2025).}
		\label{fig:forecast}
	\end{figure}
	\noindent From \Cref{fig:forecast}, it is evident that the forecast is relatively flat, with approximate incidents ranging between 651,000 and 653,000 across the projection period. Additionally, the projection starts near the 2022 peak, which reflects the influence of the sharp post-2020 rebound in contact crime figures on the ARIMA model. The CI widens over the projection period, with the lower and upper bounds being approximately 555,000 and 750,000 incidents by 2025. This widening interval is indicative of an increase in uncertainty with each projected year.  \\\\
    It should be emphasised that there are several limitations associated with the projection: (1) On a fundamental level, any forecast produced from a short series with a limited sample size of 12 data points should be treated as indicative and not definitive. This is reflected by the widening CI across the projection period; (2) The 2020 anomaly, which has been confirmed as temporary and not structural (see \Cref{trend_decomposition}), significantly influences the projection since ARIMA cannot distinguish between temporary and structural changes (\cite{box2015}). The widening CI is reflective of the volatility introduced by the COVID-19 anomaly; (3) \texttt{ARIMA(1,1,1)} itself is simple and was selected for interpretability rather than predictive precision.

	\section{Key Findings \& Policy Implications}
	Several key findings were obtained from the analysis of the South African crime statistics across RQ1 -- RQ4, with each finding having direct relevance to the national and provincial policing frameworks and priorities. 
	
	\begin{tcolorbox}[
		colback=darkgreen!8!white,
		colframe=darkgreen,
		title={\textbf{Key Findings}},
		fonttitle=\bfseries,
		rounded corners,
		breakable]
		\subsubsection*{RQ1 --- Provincial crime distribution:}
		\begin{itemize}
			\item Gauteng, Western Cape and KwaZulu-Natal recorded the highest absolute crime totals across all years, revealing a geographic concentration of reported incidents reflective of urbanisation and population size (\cite{saps2023}). The per-capita caveat was further reinforced by Northern Cape recording the lowest totals despite being the largest province by land area.
			\item The geographic concentration in reported incidents was revealed to be structural rather than temporary (\cite{saps2023}), as the provincial ranking was consistent across the cumulative and snapshot periods.
		\end{itemize}
		\subsubsection*{RQ2 --- Category trends \& prevalence:}
		\begin{itemize}
			\item Community-reported incidents dominated the national crimescape, with contact crimes recording the highest absolute crime totals across all years, accounting for approximately 41\% (\cite{iss}). Property-related and other serious crimes ranked second and third but showed an overall decline in reported incidents. Furthermore, the top five categories shared two features: a 2020 minimum and post-lockdown rebound, reflective of the COVID-19 lockdown period and subsequent lifting of restrictions (\cite{faull2021}).
			\item Police action crimes experienced an increase across the 2011 -- 2017 period, followed by a sharp decline post-2017. This behaviour could potentially be a reflection of policing intensity (additional resources, targeted operations, policing priorities) rather than genuine changes in criminal activity and public safety. 
		\end{itemize}
	\subsubsection*{RQ3 --- Cyclical and long-term patterns:}
	\begin{itemize}
		\item The underlying pattern for national contact crimes followed a broadly stable but slightly declining path for the 2011 -- 2019 period, as revealed by the moving average decomposition. Furthermore, the sharp rebound in figures post-lockdown confirmed the 2020 crime suppression to be temporary and not structural (\cite{faull2021}), as contact crimes exceeded pre-pandemic levels by 2022. 
		\item The linear trend revealed that national contact crimes remained relatively stable across the full analysis period, indicating no long-term increase or decrease.
	\end{itemize}	
	\subsubsection*{RQ4 --- Short-term forecast:}
	\begin{itemize}
		\item \texttt{ARIMA(1,1,1)} produced a nearly flat 3-year projection of national contact crime figures for 2023 -- 2025, with projected figures remaining close to the 2022 peak. This was reflective of the sharp post-2020 rebound. Additionally, the widening CI reflected the increased uncertainty from a short time series and COVID-19 anomaly.
		\item The nearly flat near-term projection suggests that, without any structural interventions, national contact crimes are unlikely to decline significantly from rebounded levels recorded in 2022. 
	\end{itemize}	
	\end{tcolorbox}
	
	\vspace{\fill}
	\noindent The key findings of the analysis are directly relevant to several existing South African policing and safety frameworks. The provincial distributions, national and categorical trend findings support the SAPS Strategic Plan 2025 -- 2030 and NDP 2030, as these findings can potentially inform the data-driven approach for high-risk area crime targeting and allocation of policing resources (\cite{ndp2030}; \cite{saps2025}). The categorical trends and provincial distribution findings support the Integrated Crime and Violence Prevention Strategy, as the relevant findings can potentially inform the crime types and areas that may require priority-level intervention. The \texttt{ARIMA(1,1,1)} projection supports the SAPS Annual Performance Plan, as the projected figures for 2023 -- 2025 can potentially guide the annual resource allocation and operational planning at a national level. All key findings in the analysis could potentially aid in the implementation of Community Policing Forums, as they could facilitate the planning of localised crime prevention initiatives and awareness campaigns through targeted crime operations.

	\section{Limitations}
    The analysis of the South African crime statistics and its findings are associated with several limitations that should be considered when interpreting results:
    
    \begin{itemize}
    	\item As mentioned in \Cref{limitations}, the dataset contains absolute crime figures and not per-capita rates, meaning the figures reflect population size rather than individual crime risk. As such, the absolute figures are not an indication of the per-capita crime risk. 
    	\item Additionally, the dataset lacks police-station level crime figures. As such, sub-provincial spatial analysis was not possible. 
    	\item Furthermore, the 2020 crime figures were temporarily suppressed due to the national lockdown. Despite having been acknowledged as an anomaly throughout the analysis, the trend decomposition and forecast results may have been influenced by its presence in the time series. 
    	\item As mentioned in \Cref{forecasting_results}, the \texttt{ARIMA(1,1,1)} was a simple model that was selected for interpretability and not precision. This was primarily reflected by the model's increased uncertainty (widening confidence interval). As such, a more complex model or a longer time series would likely produce tighter confidence intervals and more reliable projections. 
    \end{itemize}
	
	\newpage
	\section{Conclusion}
	The purpose of this analysis was to answer four structured research questions (RQ1 -- RQ4) concerning the spatial distribution, categorical composition, temporal patterns and short-term trajectory of the South African crimescape between 2011 and 2022. RQ1 revealed the structural geographic concentration of crime in densely populated and urbanised provinces. RQ2 revealed the dominance of serious community-reported crimes, with most categories being characterised by a general decline and a temporary COVID-19 anomaly. RQ3 revealed the broadly stable underlying trend of national contact crimes, with no sustained long-term increase or decrease. Finally, RQ4 revealed a nearly flat near-term projection with no immediate decline without structural intervention.\\\\
	This analysis demonstrates how publicly available structured crime data can reveal patterns not visible in annual headline figures. As such, the analysis can support existing strategic frameworks such as the NDP 2030 safety mandate and SAPS data-driven approach in an effort to tackle South Africa's crime burden.\\\\
	Several avenues exist for future work and a more advanced analysis. These include using population-normalised per-capita rates from Stats SA data, including station-level crime figures for sub-provincial spatial analysis, and using a more complex forecasting model for tighter near-term projections.
